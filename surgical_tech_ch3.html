Why this matters in the OR: Communication failures are a leading root cause of surgical errors and sentinel events. Effective teamwork and clear communication are directly linked to patient safety outcomes.
Communication: The Core Model
Two-way process
Communication is not one-directional. It requires both a sender (person delivering message) and a receiver (person receiving message). Both parties must actively participate.
Requires skill and practice
Effective communication is a learned skill — not automatic. In healthcare, poor communication can directly harm patients.
Healthcare context
Communication with patients AND coworkers often requires an immediate or specific response — especially in the OR where urgency is frequent.
5 Elements of Communication
1
Sender
The person initiating or delivering the message; responsible for clarity and accuracy
2
Receiver
The person receiving the message; responsible for active listening and understanding
3
Message
The content, information, or idea being transmitted from sender to receiver
4
Feedback
The receiver's response; confirms the message was received and understood (or misunderstood)
5
Clarification
The process of resolving misunderstanding; asking questions to ensure accurate shared understanding
Qualities of Good Verbal Communication
Clarity
Message is easily understood; precise words; no ambiguity. Critical in OR for instrument requests and critical information relay.
Neutrality
Unbiased, non-emotional tone; free from judgment; especially important with difficult situations or interpersonal conflict
Conciseness
Brief and to the point; no unnecessary information; important in fast-paced surgical setting where time is critical
Civility
Respectful, courteous tone regardless of stress level; maintains professional environment and team morale
Assertiveness
State needs and concerns directly and confidently without aggression; critical for patient safety — speak up when you see a problem
Right person, right time, right place
Consider who needs to receive this information, when it is appropriate, and where (private vs. public setting) to deliver it
Follows chain of command
Communicates through proper reporting channels; escalates concerns to appropriate supervisor
Maintains eye contact
Demonstrates attentiveness and respect; reinforces engagement; signals you are focused on the receiver
Nonverbal Communication & Body Language
Even if a person does not want to express their true feelings, those feelings will likely be conveyed through body language. Actions speak louder than words in the OR.
Examples of body language in the OR:
✦ Eye contact (or lack thereof) — attentiveness vs. dismissal
✦ Facial expressions — convey emotion; patients read these closely
✦ Posture — open (arms uncrossed) = approachable; closed = defensive
✦ Gestures — hand movements emphasize or clarify spoken message
✦ Proximity — appropriate personal space; comfort vs. intrusion
✦ Touch — can reassure or invade depending on context
✦ Tone of voice — even without words, how you say something communicates feeling
Therapeutic touch
Becoming more popular as part of a holistic approach to patient care; intentional touch to comfort and communicate care to the patient (e.g., hand-holding during pre-op); must be culturally sensitive
Qualities of good communication overall: Successful spoken communication + Good listening skills + Effective handwritten communication. All three matter in perioperative documentation and handoffs.
Communication Barriers
Perceptions — how each person interprets information based on their own framework; two people can receive the same message differently
Bias — prejudice or preconceived assumptions about a person; distorts how messages are sent and received
Environmental barriers — noise, distractions, physical distance, and equipment sounds in the OR that interfere with clear communication
Lack of understanding — educational or knowledge gaps; technical jargon not understood by all parties
Social and cultural influences — different cultural norms around eye contact, touch, authority, and directness
Emotions — stress, fear, anger, or grief interfere with the ability to send or receive information accurately
Lack of desire to communicate — disengaged team member; unwillingness to share critical information
Verbal blocking — interrupting, dismissing, or shutting down the speaker before the full message is delivered
Verbal Abuse in the Workplace
Verbal abuse is destructive to the team, to patient care, and to the professional environment. It must be recognized and reported.
Vulgar remarks directed toward staff members
Violent public criticism and deliberate demeaning of another person
Loud and abrasive comments or demands (including "surgeon rage")
Comments intended to deliberately embarrass or hurt another person
Sexual remarks and innuendo directed toward others
Problem Behaviors
Complaining
Legitimate complaining addresses a real issue and seeks resolution. Chronic complaining is persistent negativity without solution-seeking; demoralizes the team. Coping: listen briefly, redirect to solutions.
Criticism
Positive criticism: specific, nonjudgmental, focused on the task — constructive and professional. Negative criticism: personal, vague, humiliating — harmful to relationships and morale.
Gossip and rumors
Sharing unverified or private information; damages professional relationships, trust, and morale; can violate patient privacy (HIPAA)
Groupthink
Collective behavior and thinking where team members prioritize harmony/conformity over critical evaluation; dangerous in surgical settings because errors go unchallenged and unspoken concerns are suppressed
Sexual Harassment
Definition
Unwelcome sexual advances, requests for sexual favors, and other verbal or physical conduct of a sexual nature that affects employment conditions or creates a hostile, intimidating, or offensive work environment. Both men and women can be perpetrators or victims.
Coping with sexual harassment
✦ Clearly and firmly tell the person the behavior is unwelcome
✦ Document incidents (date, time, location, witnesses, what was said/done)
✦ Report to supervisor or HR following chain of command
✦ Know your institution's policy and your legal rights
✦ Federal law (Title VII) prohibits sexual harassment in the workplace
Characteristics of Good Teamwork
🤝
Yielding
Willingness to defer or compromise; recognize when another team member's input or approach is best for the patient; not placing ego above the team goal
🔄
Flexibility
Adapting to changing conditions, emergencies, and unexpected situations in the OR; willingness to adjust roles and tasks as needed without resistance
🌟
Civility
Treating every team member with respect regardless of title or stress level; maintains the professional environment even under pressure; reduces medical errors linked to disruptive behavior
💬
Collaboration
Working jointly toward shared patient care goals; active participation of all team members; pooling knowledge, skills, and resources; closed-loop communication in the OR
Poor teamwork = poor patient outcomes. Research consistently shows that communication failures and team dysfunction are primary contributors to surgical errors, wrong-site surgery, retained foreign objects, and adverse events.
Types of Teams in the OR
Formal teams — established groups with defined roles, reporting structures, and shared goals (e.g., the surgical team for a specific procedure type or service line)

Informal teams — spontaneously formed groups collaborating on a specific task; may shift based on case needs or emergency response
Interdisciplinary teams — professionals from multiple disciplines working toward a common patient care goal; typical of the full OR team (surgery, nursing, anesthesia, ST, radiology, etc.)

High-reliability teams — trained to perform consistently at a high level; anticipate problems; use standardized communication protocols (SBAR, time-out)
Team Conflict & Resolution
Interpersonal conflict
Conflict between individual team members; may stem from personality differences, stress, or communication breakdown; must be managed professionally; never allow it to affect patient care
Team goals vs. personal goals
Tension when individual priorities (e.g., finishing on time, personal preferences) conflict with team/patient care goals; patient safety always takes precedence over personal interests
Role confusion
Unclear boundaries between team member responsibilities; can lead to tasks being missed or duplicated; resolved through clear role definition, orientation, and communication
Conflict resolution strategies
Address conflict privately and promptly; use "I" statements; focus on the issue, not the person; follow chain of command if unresolved; document when necessary; mediation through management if needed
Computers in the Perioperative Setting
Patient charts & records (EHR)
Electronic health records; pre-op assessments, consent, allergies, lab values, surgical documentation — all accessed and recorded digitally in the perioperative setting
Preference cards
Surgeon-specific digital cards listing instrument sets, supplies, sutures, positioning, and preferences for each procedure type; ST uses these to set up the back table correctly
Diagnostic imaging equipment
Intraoperative imaging (C-arm fluoroscopy, CT, MRI) integrated with computer systems; images displayed in OR in real time for surgical guidance
Surgical navigation
Computer-based navigation systems (neuro, spine, ortho) that guide precision instrument placement using CT/MRI data overlaid on real-time patient anatomy
Tracking of supplies
Barcode/RFID tracking of surgical supplies, implants, and instrument sets; supports count documentation, implant traceability, and inventory management
Computer Hardware & Software
Hardware (physical components)
Central core unit (CPU)
The "brain" of the computer; processes all instructions; contains processor, memory (RAM), and storage
Peripherals
Input/output devices connected to the CPU: keyboard, mouse, monitor, printer, barcode scanner, touchscreen panels
Software
Operating software
Provides instructions that tell the computer what tasks to perform; OR-specific software includes EHR systems, preference card management, inventory tracking, and imaging platforms
Computer networks: Two or more connected computers that allow transfer of information. Hospital networks (LAN/WAN) connect OR scheduling, EHR, imaging, and supply management systems. The Internet (including World Wide Web) supports professional research via search engines.
Professional Email Etiquette — 11 Rules
1
Always include a clear, descriptive subject line
2
Use a professional greeting (Dear Mr./Ms./Dr. [Name])
3
Use short, clear sentences — one idea per paragraph
4
Do not forward an email without permission from the original author
5
Create a group/distribution list when emailing more than four recipients
6
Compress large attachments before sending to avoid delivery problems
7
Avoid unnecessary attachments — only include what is needed
8
Flaming (hostile, aggressive language) is never appropriate in professional email
9
Remember: an email can be a legal document — write accordingly
10
Always spell-check; do not use SMS abbreviations (no "u" for "you," "gr8," etc.)
11
Include your full name and contact information in your signature
Flaming = sending hostile, offensive, or inflammatory messages electronically. This is never acceptable in a professional healthcare setting and can have legal and disciplinary consequences.
Cultural Competence
Definition: The ability to communicate effectively with people of different cultures and subcultures within populations.
Why it matters in the OR:
✦ Patients from diverse backgrounds have different communication styles, beliefs about healthcare, and attitudes toward informed consent
✦ Cultural norms affect eye contact, touch, modesty, and authority dynamics
✦ Language barriers require interpreter services — not ad-hoc staff interpretation for surgical consent
✦ Religious or cultural beliefs may affect blood transfusion consent, diet post-op, or presence of family in the OR

Components of cultural competence:
✦ Self-awareness of your own cultural biases
✦ Knowledge of different cultural practices and health beliefs
✦ Communication skills adapted to each patient's context
✦ Respectful curiosity — ask rather than assume
Putting It All Together: Communication for Patient Safety
Speak up culture
Every team member — including the ST — has a responsibility to voice safety concerns. Silence in the face of a potential error is not acceptable. Assertiveness protects patients.
Closed-loop communication
Sender gives message → Receiver acknowledges → Sender confirms. Used for medication requests, critical counts, and safety checks to prevent miscommunication.
SBAR
Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation — standardized handoff communication tool; ensures complete information transfer between providers
Surgical time-out
Mandatory pre-incision communication check; confirms patient identity, procedure, site, and consent; all team members must pause and verbally confirm — the ST is part of this process
Handwritten communication
Legible, accurate documentation; intraoperative records are legal documents; sponge/needle/instrument counts must be documented clearly and in real time
